Yellowstone National Park

About Yellowstone National Park

Yellowstone National Park is more than a natural landmark — it’s a symbol of America’s commitment to preserving the wild and wonderful. Established in 1872 as the first national park in the world, Yellowstone spans over 2.2 million acres across northwest Wyoming and extends into Montana and Idaho. With its vast wilderness, unique geothermal features, and abundant wildlife, the park stands as one of the most remarkable and diverse ecosystems on Earth.

At the heart of Yellowstone lies the Yellowstone Caldera, a massive volcanic system that fuels the park’s world-famous geysers and hot springs. Iconic features like Old Faithful, Grand Prismatic Spring, and the Lower Falls of the Yellowstone River draw millions of visitors each year. But beyond the well-known sites are hundreds of miles of trails, pristine rivers, alpine lakes, and backcountry areas waiting to be explored.

Yellowstone is home to a rich variety of wildlife, including bison, elk, grizzly bears, wolves, and bald eagles. It is one of the few places in the continental United States where such animals roam freely in their natural habitat. This incredible biodiversity is part of what makes the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem one of the largest nearly intact temperate-zone ecosystems in the world.

Beyond its natural beauty, Yellowstone also holds deep cultural and historical significance. For thousands of years, Indigenous peoples have lived in and traveled through this region. Today, the park honors that history while continuing to be a place of scientific research, education, and recreation.

Whether you’re visiting to hike through vast pine forests, watch wildlife in Lamar Valley, or marvel at the geysers of the Upper Geyser Basin, Yellowstone offers a timeless connection to the raw power and beauty of nature.

Yellowstone is not just a destination — it’s an experience. A place to reconnect, reflect, and remember what wild truly means.

Park Information

Location: Northwest Wyoming, USA

Established: March 1, 1872

Area: 3,472 square miles (8,983 square kilometers)

Elevation: 5,282 feet (1,609 meters) at the summit of Eagle Peak

Visitors per year: Approximately 4 million
